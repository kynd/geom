Sound effects — brainstorm

A set of ideas for making raymarched geometry respond to audio in ways that are more interesting than simple amplitude scaling. The goal is to find techniques where the full FFT spectrum drives something geometric or optical — not just size.

Ideas considered

  • 01 Spectral spikes on a sphere
    Map the azimuthal angle on a sphere surface to a FFT bin. The radial distance at each point on the sphere equals the base radius plus the FFT value at that angle. The result is a sphere that grows spines wherever that frequency is loud — 128 independent spike bands visible simultaneously on one surface. Elevation is tapered so poles stay smooth.
    chosen → 01-spectral-hedgehog
  • 02 Bass-driven IFS fold complexity
    Use an iterated function system (Mandelbox-style: box fold + sphere inversion) where the number of fold iterations or the fold scale parameter is driven by bass energy. At silence the geometry is a clean, symmetric blob. As bass rises the IFS folds accumulate, building fractal complexity — until at peak bass the surface is fully fractured and multi-scale.
    chosen → 02-bass-fold
  • 03 Per-pixel camera orbit displacement
    Each screen pixel computes its own ray origin, displaced from the standard camera position by an amount proportional to the FFT value at the pixel's angular position on screen. The displacement is perpendicular to the viewing axis. The result is a prismatic multi-exposure effect — each neighborhood of pixels sees the object from a slightly different vantage point, creating refractive bokeh.
    chosen → 03-per-pixel-orbit
  • 04 Temporal echo stacking
    Render the same scene four times with iTime shifted by increasing offsets. Each echo is multiplied by a decreasing weight (1.0, 0.6, 0.35, 0.18). The temporal gap between echoes scales with overall amplitude, so loud moments create wide motion smears and quiet passages snap the echoes tight. The shape effectively becomes a time-integrated exposure of itself in motion.
    chosen → 04-temporal-echo
  • 05 Ray direction warp field
    Before ray marching begins, perturb the ray direction by a smooth vector field. The field is based on a radial function of screen position, scaled by the FFT value at the corresponding frequency band. This bends the rays non-uniformly — some pixels point past the object, others curve back around. The effect resembles gravitational lensing or thick glass, with the distortion pattern driven by the spectral content of the audio.
    chosen → 05-ray-warp
  • 06 Per-column camera tilt (3D equalizer)
    Divide the screen into vertical columns. Each column maps to a FFT bin. The camera ray for that column is tilted upward by an amount proportional to the FFT value at that bin. The scene being viewed is a horizontal plane of spheres. Each column independently aims at a different height of the same sphere, producing the look of a 3D equalizer bar chart rendered in raymarched depth.
    chosen → 06-fft-columns
  • 07 Chromatic aberration by amplitude
    March three separate rays — R, G, B — with the G ray standard and the R/B rays offset by a small lateral divergence scaled by amplitude. The lateral offset direction rotates with time, so at peak loudness the RGB channels separate into a rainbow fringe. Quiet passages snap back to white. Technically clean but visually familiar — the colored-edge effect is well-known from photography.
    skipped — too familiar
  • 08 Domain repetition cell size
    Apply mod-based space repetition (sdRepeat) to a sphere or box, where the cell size for each axis is driven by a different FFT band. On kick hits the cells shrink in X (denser columns); on treble hits they shrink in Z (denser rows). The geometry becomes a grid of objects whose density encodes the spectrum.
    skipped — effect is subtle
  • 09 Noise field amplitude blend
    Blend between two smooth noise fields based on spectral centroid — a weighted average frequency of the spectrum. Low centroid (bass-heavy) selects a low-frequency blob field; high centroid (treble-heavy) selects a high-frequency detail field. The result morphs continuously between two noise textures. Smooth and atmospheric but not very dramatic at any moment.
    skipped — too subtle
  • 10 Strange attractor parameter sweep
    Render the density cloud of a Clifford or Lorenz strange attractor as an implicit surface. Drive the attractor's A/B/C parameters with different FFT bands. The orbit changes character at each value, potentially bifurcating or collapsing. Interesting for science visualization but hard to ray-march cleanly without a particle density volume; the rendering complexity is high relative to the visual payoff.
    skipped — rendering challenges
  • 11 Slit-scan time slice
    Each horizontal row of pixels is rendered at a different audio frame offset — top rows are earlier in time, bottom rows are later. The result is a slit-scan photograph of the animation through time rather than space, smearing the object's temporal history across the canvas. Closest in spirit to temporal echo but spatial/vertical rather than composite.
    skipped — overlap with 04
  • 12 FFT-driven surface normal recoloring
    Compute standard diffuse shading, then modulate the albedo color by a hue derived from the surface normal's dot product with the view vector, shifted by the dominant FFT frequency. The surface becomes a frequency-mapped color space rather than a neutral grey. Interesting as a color effect but doesn't change shape; less geometric drama.
    skipped — no geometry change
  • 13 Recursive domain zoom
    Scale the domain down by a factor each frame iteration, controlled by mid frequency. At each scale level, combine the SDF with the one at the previous level using smooth-min. Creates a fractal-zoom zoom-in effect similar to IFS folds but via progressive scale reduction rather than reflection symmetry. Visually similar to 02-bass-fold, so not selected to avoid redundancy.
    skipped — overlaps with 02
  • 14 Polar FFT extrusion to torus
    Map the 128 FFT bins onto a full circle in the XZ plane, converting each to a radial displacement on a torus. The torus minor radius oscillates per-bin based on FFT value. Visually similar to the spectral hedgehog but on torus topology — the spectrum wraps around a ring rather than a sphere. Redundant with 01 given the similar mapping strategy.
    skipped — too similar to 01
  • 15 Particle trail SDF
    Place N pseudo-particles whose positions are driven by different audio frequencies. Compute the SDF as the smooth union of N small spheres. Loud moments pull particles outward; quiet ones contract them inward. The aggregate shape changes topology based on how many spheres are touching. Interesting but requires careful distance function tuning for N > 6 to maintain marching stability.
    skipped — SDF stability risk
Self-evaluation. The six chosen ideas cover distinct mechanisms: geometry from spectrum (01), fractal complexity from bass (02), multi-view parallax (03), temporal smear (04), optical/lens distortion (05), and 3D column visualization (06). Ideas 07–15 were skipped for one of three reasons: visual effect too familiar (07), effect too subtle to be interesting on a first viewing (08, 09, 12), overlap with a stronger chosen idea (11, 13, 14), or rendering stability risk (10, 15). The key criterion for selection was whether the effect would read as surprising and geometrically distinct at first glance — not just a parameter tweak on existing shapes.
