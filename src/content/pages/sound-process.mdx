## What is an FFT?

<p>
  Sound is a pressure wave — a single signal that varies over time.
  The <strong>Fourier transform</strong> decomposes that time signal into a sum of pure sine waves,
  each at a distinct frequency.
  For a short window of audio samples, it answers: <em>how much energy is present at each frequency right now?</em>
  The result is a spectrum — a list of magnitudes, one per frequency bin.
</p>
<p>
  The <strong>Fast Fourier Transform</strong> (FFT) is an efficient algorithm for computing this decomposition.
  Applying it to overlapping windows across the full track produces a <em>Short-Time Fourier Transform</em> (STFT):
  a spectrum for each frame, giving the time-varying bar graph you see above.
  Low-frequency content (bass, kick) appears on the left; high-frequency content (cymbals, air) on the right.
</p>

## Log transform

<p>
  On a linear scale, loud transients dominate and quiet detail disappears.
  Human hearing is roughly logarithmic — equal steps of perceived loudness correspond to
  multiplicative changes in physical intensity, not additive ones.
</p>
<p>
  Applying this formula to a normalised value {"\\("}x \in [0, 1]{"\\)"}:
</p>
<div class="math-block">{"\\["} y = \frac&#123;\log(1 + k \cdot x)&#125;&#123;\log(1 + k)&#125; {"\\]"}</div>
<p>
  boosts the low end while compressing the top, keeping the output in {"\\("}[0, 1]{"\\)"}.
  At {"\\("}k = 100{"\\)"} (the setting used here), a value of 0.01 maps to roughly 0.33 —
  three times what the linear scale would show.
  Toggle the <strong>log</strong> button in the demo to compare the two views.
</p>
